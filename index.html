🧑‍💼 Candidate 1: Daniel — The Bad Interview

📄 Read Daniel's interview script below. As you read, think about what he does wrong and why it makes a bad impression.
📋 Daniel's Interview Script
Webware — Sales Director Interview
Daniel: Hi there.
Marcia: Good morning, Daniel.
Daniel: Oh, Dan, please. ⚠️ Too informal — use your full name in a formal interview.
Marcia: I'm Marcia Baldwin, we spoke on the phone, and this is Philip Hart, our CEO.
Phillip: Hello.
Daniel: Nice to meet you both.
Phillip: So, it says here on your CV that you're interested in rock climbing.
Daniel: Yeah, I love it.
Phillip: Great, so do I. When did you last go climbing?
Daniel: Er, well, it was a while ago now.
Phillip: Anywhere interesting?
Daniel: Yeah, I walked up the hill behind my house. It took me about 30 minutes. ⚠️ He exaggerated on his CV.
Marcia: Okay, Daniel, can you tell us a little bit about your current position?
Daniel: Certainly. I'm part of the European sales team at Networld — we're the world's leading supplier of IT hardware.
Phillip: Why do you want to move on?
Daniel: Well, they're too small for me. ⚠️ Never criticise your current employer.
Phillip: What first attracted you to Webware in particular?
Daniel: Well, I see Webware as kind of like a stepping stone. Start here, gain the necessary experience, and then move on to something bigger. ⚠️ Never say the job is just a stepping stone!
Marcia: Let's move on to managing people. Can you give an example of dealing with a difficult member of staff?
Daniel: A couple of years ago I was working with a guy — he wasn't pulling his weight, basically being lazy, so I told him to get out. ⚠️ No warning or review — very unprofessional.
Marcia: You didn't give him a performance review or a warning, or try to develop his motivation?
Daniel: No, none of that rubbish. If someone's not working hard enough, then they're out. That's the way I work. I'm a hard man. ⚠️ Arrogant and aggressive tone.
Phillip: How would you handle a rapidly changing market?
Daniel: Basically, not change anything at all — just continue on as normal regardless. ⚠️ No flexibility or strategic thinking.
Marcia: According to your CV, you are fluent in Mandarin.
Daniel: Well, I wouldn't say fluent exactly... I like Chinese food a lot, so I can read the menu. ⚠️ He lied on his CV.
Marcia: Can you tell us about a time you had to close a particularly challenging deal?
Daniel: I haven't had one. They're all easy for me. I close deals all the time. ⚠️ Overconfident — gives no real example.
Marcia: Do you have any questions for us?
Daniel: No. ⚠️ Always prepare questions to show genuine interest!

Daniel arrives and introduces himself as "Dan". When asked about rock climbing on his CV, he admits he only walked up the hill behind his house — not very impressive.

He says he wants to leave Networld because it is "too small" for him. He sees Webware as a "stepping stone" to something bigger — not a long-term position.

When asked about managing a difficult staff member, he says he simply fired the person immediately, with no warning or performance review. He describes himself as "a hard man."

On how to handle a rapidly changing market, Daniel says he would "not change anything at all."

His CV says he is fluent in Mandarin, but he admits: "I wouldn't say fluent exactly... I can read the menu."

He says all sales deals are easy for him and he has never faced a challenging one. At the end, when asked if he has questions, he says simply: "No."

👩‍💼 Candidate 2: Sarah — The Good Interview

▶️
Watch: A Good Job Interview Example
YouTube · See the right techniques in practice · Opens in new tab
↗

Sarah does voluntary work with children who have difficulties — she finds it rewarding and challenging.

She is currently Assistant Sales Director (not Sales Director) for a chain of language schools. She believes the skills are the same despite the different product.

She wants to move on because she has come as far as she can in her current role and wants more responsibility.

She resolved a conflict between two staff members by sitting down with them and discussing their differences. Things improved after that.

She speaks French and Spanish (she has lived in both countries). She won her biggest contract with a university in India — a complex deal that she still pulled off.

At the end, she asks about the company's ethical policy and carbon footprint, and whether products are ethically sourced. The interviewers are impressed.
